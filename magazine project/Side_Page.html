Chennai wins right to host 2022 Chess Olympiad

Colin McGourty

Chennai, India will host the 44th Chess Olympiad in late July and early August 2022 after the government of Tamil Nadu guaranteed $10 million in funding to host the massive team chess event, that in 2018 featured nearly 1,700 participants. The 2022 Olympiad was originally granted to Belarus, then moved to Russia, and now in the wake of Russia’s war on Ukraine has been moved to India. 15th World Chess Champion Vishy Anand, a resident of Chennai, has been an enthusiastic supporter of the bid.

There's nothing quite like a Chess Olympiad! | photo: Alina l'Ami, 2018 Batumi Olympiad

The 43rd Chess Olympiad was held in Batumi, Georgia in 2018, with China winning both the Open and Women’s event. The tournament had been held every two years since 1972, but in 2020, when the event was planned for Russia, the pandemic intervened, with the massive participation event logistically impossible. An Online Olympiad was held instead, with the most unlikely of endings when Russia and India were declared joint gold medalists after internet issues hit the final.

Nevertheless, the gold medals for the rising chess powerhouse of India were widely celebrated, including by Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi.

The World Chess Federation FIDE was tempted by the success of that event to hold a 2nd Online Olympiad in 2021, where Russia this time finished clear first, ahead of the USA (silver) and India (bronze), but the experience wasn’t comparable to the chess celebration of hundreds of countries from all over the world meeting up in one venue.

India and Russia's controversial joint gold is not likely to be repeated anytime soon! | image: FIDE

As the pandemic has begun to recede, however, there has been more turmoil. In October 2018 it was announced that Minsk, Belarus would host the 2022 Olympiad, but in 2020 Belarus were stripped of the Olympiad. FIDE talked about financial obligations not being met, but holding the Olympiad in Belarus looked inconceivable in the wake of mass protests against the alleged falsification of the 2020 presidential elections.

The 2022 Olympiad was instead given to Russia (the planned hosts for 2020), whose President Vladimir Putin had been instrumental in shoring up Alexander Lukashenko’s government in Belarus. Originally planned for Khanty-Mansiysk, the event was moved to Moscow, but then, last month, Russia invaded Ukraine, partly from Belarusian territory, and holding the Olympiad in Russia became unthinkable overnight. FIDE soon announced that Russia had been stripped of the right to host the Olympiad.

That left FIDE needing to find a last-minute host for the most difficult and expensive of all chess events, but fortunately this is where India stepped up. A huge chess boom has seen India go from having just Vishy Anand as a grandmaster to having 73 grandmasters in the space of a few decades, with India already ranked 4th among chess federations (behind Russia, USA and China) before its army of young stars has yet reached their true potential.

The growing popularity of the game of chess in what most historians consider the country that created it has created great potential for sponsorship. In 2013, the then Chief Minister of Tamil Nadu J Jayalithaa provided the backing for the Anand-Carlsen World Chess Championship match to be played in Chennai, and in 2022 her successor, M.K. Stalin, announced that Chennai would host the Olympiad.

He later called the event “a milestone in the history of Tamil Nadu”.

15th World Chess Champion Vishy Anand had supported the bid.

“Vanakam” is “hello”, or “welcome” in Tamil.

The amount of money required to host an event such as the Olympiad was made clear by the Tamil Nadu Government guaranteeing $10 million in funds to cover expenses.

The Olympiad will take place in the convention centre at the Four Points by Sheraton hotel, located in the south of Chennai, with the exact dates still to be confirmed.

The holding of the Chess Olympiad in India has been greeted with delight by the Indian chess community.

Chess players from all over the world will also be excited at the chance to visit India, though there are still shadows on the horizon. The pandemic isn’t over, and massive lockdowns, for instance in China, could still interfere with the tournament — as noted, the Chinese teams are the reigning champions in both the Open and Women’s sections.

The other major issue is the war in Ukraine. It looks inconceivable that the Russian teams would be allowed to play, even if the war did end in the near future, with Belarus likely to come under the same sanctions. Will Ukraine be able to field teams? Will war or uncertainty spread? At the moment India finds itself in the near impossible position of trying to maintain good relations with both Russia and the USA/Europe.

Chennai, India will be holding its 2nd massive chess event after the 2013 Anand-Carlsen World Championship match

And then there’s the question of FIDE Elections, which traditionally are held alongside the Olympiad every four years. Former Russian Deputy President Arkady Dvorkovich was elected in 2018 in Batumi. His Russian ties had ensured healthy funding for FIDE projects, but the war has seen him face demands to resign, not least from Ukrainian chess players.

In a US interview Dvorkovich seemed to take personal risks by condemning the war and supporting restrictions on Russian players. That saw him called a “son of a bitch” by Dmitry Rogozin, another former Deputy Prime Minister of Russia and the head of the Russian space program. Rogozin has been one of the most vocal supporters of Vladimir Putin in his latest war.

Whether because of that, or simply since he was addressing a different audience, Arkady Dvorkovich made a shocking statement as Head of the Skolkovo Science Park outside of Moscow. He criticised sanctions and companies leaving Russia and adopted the language of state propaganda when he “clarified” what he meant by being against war.

Removing Nazis and bringing peace were among Putin's absurd justifications for unleashing massive destruction on a normal, democratic country. If the FIDE President was quoted accurately it must be in great doubt whether he can continue in his role until the next presidential elections during the Olympiad. If he did, we might also get boycotts of the Olympiad.

Nevertheless, we have to hope that the 2022 Olympiad will be what Olympiads traditionally are — a celebration of the diversity and sense of community of the chess world. Chennai has the potential to be a wonderful venue.
